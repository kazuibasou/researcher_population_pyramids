About

This site provides an interactive view of researcher demographics and gender balance across 58 countries, based on the population pyramid framework introduced in our paper (Kazuki Nakajima and Takayuki Mizuno (2026), PNAS Nexus). The full methodology is described in the paper; this page summarizes what each view shows, defines the key terms, and lists interpretive caveats.

The site has three visualizations:

  • Pyramid: the researcher population pyramid for a chosen country, year, and research domain — active authors of each gender plotted by their cumulative productivity.
  • Trends: time series of active-author counts and the proportion of female active authors for selected countries.
  • Comparison: every country positioned by its researcher inflow against its gender gap in cumulative productivity at a chosen year.

Years 2024–2050 are projected from the 2023 pyramid (see Projection below).

Key concepts

Active author

For each country and gender, we set an inter-publication interval threshold as the largest inter-publication interval (in years) at which the survival probability remains above 2%. An author is considered active in a given year if their most recent publication on or before that year falls within this threshold of the year’s end. Authors who do not meet this criterion are considered inactive.

In addition, an author is included in our pyramids only if (i) they have published at least one paper by that year, (ii) the duration between their first publication and the end of that year does not exceed 40 years, and (iii) their total number of publications by that year is at most 500 (approximately the top 0.1% of authors in our data).

Cumulative productivity

For each active author, cumulative productivity is the length of their most recent, uninterrupted sequence of publications. The count starts at 1 with the first publication and increases by one with each subsequent publication whose gap from the previous one does not exceed the inter-publication interval threshold; an interval exceeding the threshold restarts the count at 1 with the next publication.

Researcher inflow

Researcher inflow in a given year is the proportion of newly active authors — those who were inactive the previous year but become active that year — among all active authors.

Gender gap in cumulative productivity

The gender gap in cumulative productivity is defined as the mean cumulative productivity of female active authors minus that of male active authors, normalized by the male mean.

Data

Publication and author records are from the September 27, 2024 snapshot of OpenAlex, from which we extracted publications classified as “articles” and published between 1950 and 2023. For each author, a primary country (or countries) of affiliation is assigned as the most frequent country among their affiliations. Authors are kept only when their primary country of affiliation is unique.

The 58 countries are those for which the paper’s Complement Naive Bayes classifier achieved an area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUC) of at least 0.8 on both the Orbis executive database and the World Gender Name Dictionary benchmarks. The classifier takes the first space-separated token of an author’s full name (assumed to be the first name) together with their country and outputs a binary gender; the gender is assigned only when the classifier’s confidence exceeds a country-specific threshold.

Bangladesh, China, India, and South Korea are not included due to insufficient classifier accuracy on the WGND benchmark. This is partially consistent with recent work demonstrating that the error rates of name-based gender inference are not uniformly distributed (J. W. Lockhart, M. M. King, and C. Munsch (2023), Nature Human Behaviour).

Note on the classifier. The 58 countries listed above are those validated in the paper using a Complement Naive Bayes classifier. This site applies the open-source nomquamgender classifier (I. Van Buskirk, A. Clauset, and D. B. Larremore (2023), ICWSM) using the country-specific confidence thresholds shown in the paper’s Supplementary Information. Numerical values on this site may therefore differ from those reported in the paper at the country level.

The Pyramid page also allows filtering by one of OpenAlex’s four top-level research domains (Health Sciences, Life Sciences, Physical Sciences, and Social Sciences). An author contributes to a domain only if all of their publications fall within that domain; authors spanning multiple domains are counted only in “Overall”.

Projection

The population pyramid for years beyond 2023 is propagated forward from the 2023 pyramid using the transition probabilities of cumulative productivity observed between 2022 and 2023 together with the newly active author counts at 2023. Under a stationarity assumption, these are held fixed and applied iteratively to obtain projected pyramids for every year up to 2050.

This deterministic projection serves as a baseline scenario illustrating long-term demographic trajectories if 2023 conditions were to persist. It does not account for cohort aging, policy shifts, or external shocks, and the resulting projections should be interpreted as stylized illustrations of current momentum rather than predictions.

Interpretive caveats

  • A career interruption that exceeds the inter-publication interval threshold (e.g., due to parenthood) resets the cumulative productivity count to one. This may shape the interpretation of gender gaps in cumulative productivity.
  • Gender is treated as binary (female or male) inferred from first names. This approach cannot account for individuals who identify as non-binary, transgender, or otherwise gender-diverse, and is an imperfect proxy for gender identity.
  • Gender assignment rates — the proportion of unique authors for whom a binary gender was inferred with high confidence — vary considerably across countries. For countries with lower rates the gender-assigned subset may not be representative of the entire researcher population.
  • Projections to 2050 are based on a strong stationarity assumption that the inflow in 2023 and the transition patterns observed between 2022 and 2023 remain constant in the future.
